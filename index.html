The Conveyance Manifesto: 

Compassionate Design,

Resourceful Minimalism, 

and Guiding by Example

By Az (@HereLiesAz)

Introduction: You Got the Gift

Today is yours. Everything is going your way.

And sometimes, that's actually true. You are in the zone! Things just fall into your lap, you have a sudden, prodigious mastery of the world in front of you, and even your big dick energy has big dick energy.

How wonderful is that, having near-superhuman powers? What a gift!

And, more often than you know, it is a gift. Someone conspired to give you that moment. It wasn't easy, making everything feel so easy. Authorities on inter-generational relationships call it a mother’s intuition. In law enforcement, they call it entrapment. In poker, they call it beginner’s luck. In design, it’s called conveyance. And it means you’ve been set-up and manipulated to specific responses, no matter what you call it. Not easy at all! But that you got to feel like it was—absolutely worth all the planning it took.

Where that sort of manipulation has the opportunity to be completely positive is in interactive design. Not every example allows someone to interact with the thing that is designed, so designers in fields where interaction is possible–architects, software designers, city planning commissions,  etc.–have a unique opportunity. And though you may not recognize it, because your creations necessarily manipulate people into specific actions, I will say you have a unique responsibility, too.

Convey what people can do and how to do it. 

That doesn’t mean leaving a note or audio tour. As Ikea and lawyers everywhere have helped us realize, instructions are not the answer. Plus, you aren’t a writer. You have no business handling words. You’re a designer, so design. Fulfill that obligation and responsibility by simply engaging your design.

Not all video game designers manage to do this, which might be confusing–you figure out a game by playing it, right? Well, think about Solitaire in the hands of someone who’s never seen it played. If someone sits down and tells you the rules and shows you how to play, you might get it, but even the best explanations won’t include everything you need to know, like how to not lose. And many of the rules don’t really follow from other rules, or from the components of the game. In baseball, it looks like an entire team is playing against one person. That guy hit it really far and he started running but then went back to hit the ball again. Hits it again, and was told he was out. Why? Nothing about the game explains how to play it. I remember Superman on the Nintendo64 driving me crazy. I’d spent my whole allowance on this game (it was the only one I could afford), and to this day I don’t think the objective was anything more than flying through some rings. None of the controls felt right, so even that was  incredibly hard to do. And neither success nor failure taught me anything about succeeding or failing. Frankly, the game had nothing to do with Superman, so there was no reward of any kind in trying either way.

That’s bad conveyance.

By contrast, there are games that, even when playing them for the first time, it all simply clicks. Nobody hands you a manual. No text boxes pop up to explain the rules. Yet, somehow, you know exactly what to do. The environment subtly guides your hand. Doing something instantly confirms you are on the right track and naturally leads you to what’s next. You don't feel you are being taught; you feel like you’re just doing it, and you know how to do it. It is an invisible, effortless flow that makes you feel smart, competent, and completely in control.

I have Arin Hanson to thank for explaining all of this, and his example of excellent conveyance is Megaman X. Classic Megaman does it too. Portal and Portal 2 are pretty good at conveying what needs to be done in the same way. Another game, Stanley: a Postmodern Parable, was explicitly designed to play with the concept and leave you feeling nihilistic in the process.

Most people absolutely recognize this feeling when they experience it. Unfortunately, experiencing it is incredibly rare.

Our current approach to User Interface (UI) and User Experience (UX) design often treats users like idiots who will inevitably get lost without a barrage of explicit instructions, tooltips, pop-ups, and static signposts. It is time to abandon this high-friction environment. We must replace it with a philosophy built on two foundational pillars. The first is the formal name for that feeling of effortless, intuitive guidance: Conveyance (leading by example). The second is Resourceful Minimalism (putting every present element to work).

The M3 Expressive Accident

To understand how to successfully implement conveyance in visual design, we can look at a recent, highly instructive accident in the industry: Google’s Material 3 (M3) Expressive design system.

M3 Expressive was introduced as an evolution of the Material 3 system, utilizing new components, fluid motion, and shape morphing to create emotionally impactful UX1. Through focus groups and extensive research, Google discovered that users responded incredibly well to these updates. However, the explanation for why it worked was misplaced, and led them to things to which people didn’t respond so well. Google concluded that the design succeeded because components "felt alive," utilizing a new motion physics system that replaces static duration and easing with spring-based, fluid animations2. It’s all very expressive.

They were so close!

Google’s designers intentionally layered these expressive tactics to create what they call a "hero moment"—a brief, delightful, and unexpected interaction meant to frame essential information in a fresh way1. But their reasoning often centers around getting and maintaining the user's attention. Google's own research on Material 3 Expressive found that participants were able to spot key UI elements up to four times faster when these highly expressive, dynamic tactics were used5. But finding a button quickly is not the same thing as intuitively understanding the systemic rules of the environment.

The reason the focus groups loved the "hero moment" was not simply because the UI looked organic or biological, or because it grabbed their attention. The real magic was Conveyance.

When an M3 Expressive component behaves as if it is alive—such as a button group morphing into non-static shapes for as long as a loading animation is required, or a Floating Action Button (FAB) seamlessly expanding into a full menu2—what it is doing tells the user exactly what they just did, and intuitively guides them toward what they need to do next. It’s hard to follow the example of something that isn't alive, which is why Google’s explanation works some of the time. Those elements guide by example.

The Construction Zone Epidemic

Basically every single digital design out there looks to me the same way I see road construction.

There are a lot of signs up, giving instructions and warnings, cautionary tales of tape and cones, a lot of fluorescent day-glow colors doing their best to be obvious, but at some point that wasn’t  enough, so they added blinking lights and posted speed limit changes.

At the core of it all are a group of construction workers.

They look like they're waiting for something, but if they are, they're in no hurry for it to come. Or in no hurry whatsoever. They're all just standing around except for one guy, digging a hole, the only worker doing any work.

Now, I’m not a roadside construction worker and I definitely don’t know what I’m talking about here, as far as construction-working goes. But I know I’m not alone in thinking, “If you're not working, why are you there? Go home. Or better yet, get to work!”

What this has to do with conveyance, is the necessity for resourceful minimalism. In your designs, if something is there, put it to work. Maximize its utility and get rid of anything that's "just standing around, doing nothing." A well-placed construction vehicle can do more than miles of traffic cones and cautionary tape.

To continue the metaphor, Google’s explanations behind M3 Expressive make it the construction worker that's waving their hands to direct traffic. And because they think the goal is about getting people's attention, Google-the-construction-worker does a strip tease. This absolutely grabs everyone's attention. It's still up in the air whether people actually comprehend Google's simultaneous traffic directing. Fingers crossed that they don't miss it, even though they're looking right at him.

Aesthetics as Distraction

It is important to acknowledge that there are some very, beautifully designed apps and websites out there. And they have to be beautiful to distract the user from the absolute mess.

"To your left, yes, there's road construction. You may see Google do a strip tease. But if you look to your right, you have the best view of the sun setting into the Grand Canyon."

Visual beauty—high-resolution imagery, trendy layouts, and complex animations—to hide the underlying friction and lack of intuitive routing does not remove the friction or unintuitive routing.

        True conveyance dictates that aesthetics must serve a functional purpose. In Google's Material 3 Expressive system, the design guidelines explicitly state that expressive tactics—such as applying rich, nuanced colors, bold typography, or fluid motion—must be used to prioritize actions, simplify navigation, and naturally guide the user's focus1. Why didn’t they leave off the word “focus”, encouraging users to naturally guide the user? The aesthetic beauty of a design should be the exact mechanism that intuitively leads the user forward. If a beautiful UI element is just sitting on the screen looking pretty without actively helping the user understand how the system operates, it is failing the rule of resourceful minimalism.

The Core Principle: Resourceful Minimalism

The discovery of the hero moment in M3 Expressive highlights another vital principle: resourceful minimalism. In M3 Expressive, a single element often performs more than one related function. A split button spins and changes shape when activated, serving as both a touchpoint and a status indicator2. A loading indicator replaces indeterminate circular progress bars by integrating directly into the pull-to-refresh behavior2.

In a prescriptive design ideology, resourceful minimalism dictates that if a visual element exists on the screen, its utility must be maximized. If you have a button, it should not just be a static rectangle waiting to be clicked. When interacted with, it should morph, guide, and react. We must eliminate anything in the interface that is "just standing around, doing nothing."

A Prescriptive Guide to Visual Conveyance

To achieve true conveyance and resourceful minimalism in your own visual designs, adhere to the following ideological steps:

1. Eradicate Explicit Instruction

Stop relying on text-heavy onboarding screens, tooltips, and static warning labels to teach users how to navigate your software. If you have to explain how an interface works with a paragraph of text, the design has failed. Rely instead on cognitive affordances—design cues that make the function of an object intuitively obvious. So, you will need to plot every single user-flow, edit it down to only the necessary, and meticulously plan your design.

2. Double-Up Your Efforts

Consolidate your UI. Do not use three separate elements (a button, a loading bar, and a success checkmark) when one element can seamlessly transition through all three states. If a component is on the screen, force it to pull its weight. Maximize the utility of every pixel.

3. Guide by Example (Systemic Conveyance)

Use motion, shape, and contrast to teach the user the rules of your digital environment.

  • Shape and Morphing: Use a variety of shapes and corner radii to generate visual tension and direct focus without pointing a literal arrow at the objective4.

  • Dynamic Color: Use contrasting primary, secondary, and tertiary tones to prioritize actions implicitly3.

  • Physics and Motion: When a user interacts with a component, the physical reaction of that component should demonstrate its purpose. The animation should convey the consequence of the action immediately, creating a feedback loop that trains the user's muscle memory.

4. Engineer the Hero Moment

Invest your time in making your most critical interactions sing1. A hero moment should not be a superfluous animation; it should be the ultimate manifestation of conveyance. It is the moment where the interface perfectly responds to the user, proving that the system understands their intent and is actively guiding them forward. Limit these moments to the emotional and functional core of your product to avoid overwhelming the user.

The Core Philosophy: Compassionate Design

Underpinning conveyance and resourceful minimalism is a profound commitment to the user. In the realm of UX design, it is crucial to recognize the distinct difference between empathy and compassion. Empathy is the ability to understand and share the feelings or frustrations of a user6. But empathy is a passive state. Compassion is the active desire and ensuing action taken to alleviate that frustration and improve the user's well-being7.

Academic and professional definitions of Compassionate Design center on three core pillars: paying special attention to the user's sense of dignity, empowerment, and security9. The principles of this manifesto align perfectly with these pillars:

  • Dignity: Dignity refers to a sense of self and the recognition of the user as an individual9. The current "construction zone" approach treats people like idiots. True Compassionate Design respects the user's intelligence. By removing patronizing, text-heavy instructions and trusting the user's intrinsic ability to observe and learn, the design honors their dignity.

  • Empowerment: Empowerment refers to giving the user a sense of control and competence in a situation9. When an interface utilizes conveyance and resourceful minimalism—where a single component fluidly guides the user to the next step—it empowers the user to navigate the system intuitively. They feel like they are mastering the tool, rather than fighting against it.

  • Security and Alleviating Cognitive Fatigue: Security refers to the user's sense of safety and assessment of risk9. A chaotic UI full of traffic cones and flashing lights creates a high-friction, stressful environment that drains a user's energy. By engineering dynamic elements that "lead by example" rather than just shouting for attention, you are actively removing the user's cognitive burden. Eliminating this fatigue is the ultimate goal of compassionate action7.

Conclusion: Give Them the Benefit of the Doubt

At the heart of the construction zone epidemic is a lack of trust. Designers clutter screens with warnings and instructions because they assume the user will fail without them.

If you treat people like idiots, they will not let you down.

Give your users the benefit of the doubt. Trust their intrinsic ability to observe, learn, and follow an example. By implementing pedagogical conveyance through fluid, responsive interactions, and by ruthlessly applying literal resourceful minimalism, you strip away the noise. You are not just making the software prettier or more efficient; you are actively designing with compassion for the human on the other side of the screen. Your website will cease to be a chaotic, frustrating construction site, and will instead become a unified, intuitive tool where every element is actively digging the hole.
